Complete Buying Guide: How to Choose

1. Seat Height Range

Measure from the floor to behind your knees while sitting. Your chair should adjust from 2 inches below this to 2 inches above. Most budget chairs handle 17"-21" (good for people 5'4" to 6'2").

2. Lumbar Support Reality

90% of "ergonomic" chairs under $150 have fake lumbar support - just a slight curve in the backrest. Real lumbar support pushes into your lower back. Test: lean back and see if you feel pressure at your belt line.

3. Armrest Importance

Fixed armrests cause shoulder strain. Look for height adjustment minimum. Width and angle adjustment are nice bonuses but not critical under $200.

4. Weight Capacity Truth

Manufacturers inflate these numbers. Subtract 50 lbs from the "official" capacity for realistic long-term use.

Frequently Asked Questions

How long do budget office chairs actually last?

Honest answer: 2-4 years with daily use. The chairs I recommend will give you the full 4 years. Avoid anything under $120 - those rarely make it past 18 months.

Are expensive chairs really worth 4x the price?

For most people, no. A $200 chair gets you 80% of the comfort and ergonomics of a $800 chair. The extra $600 buys premium materials, longer warranty, and brand prestige - not dramatically better comfort.

Should I buy a gaming chair for office work?

Generally no. Gaming chairs prioritize looks over ergonomics. The bucket seat design that's great for racing isn't ideal for typing. Stick to office chairs for office work.

What about used Herman Miller or Steelcase chairs?

Great option if you can find them locally. Check Facebook Marketplace, OfferUp, and office liquidation sales. Just test before buying - worn-out expensive chairs can be worse than new budget ones.
